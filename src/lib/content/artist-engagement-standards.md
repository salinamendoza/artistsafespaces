# Artist Engagement Standards
 
**How Artist Safespaces engages the artists we work with.**
 
We publish these standards because most artist agreements are written to protect the institution. Ours are written to protect the artist. If you're working with us — at a festival, on a mural, in an Art Park, on a print run — these are the terms. They are not negotiated downward. Sponsors, partners, and cities adjust to them, not the other way around.
 
---
 
## 1. Pay before the work, never after
 
Artists are paid before any creative labor begins, or on the day of the event at the latest. **Never after.**
 
We don't wait for sponsor checks to clear, invoice cycles to close, or "post-event reconciliation." Artist Safespaces absorbs all timing risk between sponsor pay schedules and artist pay. If a sponsor pays us net-60, that is our problem to manage — not the artist's.
 
For multi-stage work (e.g., murals with design and production phases), payment is structured so the artist is never out-of-pocket on materials or labor at any point in the project.
 
## 2. Market rate, always
 
Every artist is paid at standard market rate for their discipline and the scope of the work. Rates are non-negotiable downward — we will not ask an artist to work below market rate "for exposure," "for the cause," or "for the relationship." If a partner cannot fund market-rate compensation, the activity does not happen.
 
This applies equally to lead muralists, performing poets, DJs, photographers, screen printers, festival artists, workshop facilitators, and anyone else whose creative labor we engage.
 
## 3. Artists keep their work, their rights, and their name
 
**No moral rights waivers.** Ever. Artists retain full authorship, attribution, and the right to object to distortion, modification, or recontextualization of their work. The Visual Artists Rights Act protections we have under U.S. law are floors, not ceilings — we go beyond them, not around them.
 
**Attribution is required, not optional.** Every public-facing use of an artist's work — photo, video, print, social, sponsor reporting — credits the artist by name. Always.
 
**Artists own their work.** Original pieces, files, and IP remain the artist's property. Artist Safespaces does not claim ownership of any work created at our events or commissioned through our programming.
 
## 4. Limited license, plainly defined
 
When we engage an artist, we ask for one specific license: the right to use documentation of their work — photos, video, footage — to promote the event the work was made for, and to promote Artist Safespaces' nonprofit mission, in perpetuity.
 
That's the whole license. To make it concrete, here's what we **can** do:
 
- Use photos of an artist's mural from Art Therapy 2026 forever, in case studies, sponsor pitches, social media, our website, and grant applications
- Reference the work in nonprofit promotional materials, press, and franchise/chapter activations of Artist Safespaces
- Share documentation with the specific event sponsor for their reporting on the event they sponsored
Here's what we **cannot** do:
 
- License the work to third-party brands for unrelated marketing campaigns
- Sell, sublicense, or transfer the work or its documentation
- Use the work to promote anything that isn't Artist Safespaces' nonprofit mission
- Modify the work or present it in a way the artist would object to
## 5. No exclusivity
 
Working with Artist Safespaces never restricts an artist's ability to sell, show, license, or work elsewhere. We don't ask for first-look rights, non-competes, or any restrictions on an artist's other engagements.
 
## 6. Sponsor co-marketing is opt-in, brokered, and direct
 
If a sponsor (e.g., IKEA, a future corporate partner) wants to feature an artist's work in **their** marketing — beyond the event documentation license above — that is a separate paid deal between the artist and the sponsor.
 
How it works: Artist Safespaces brokers the introduction. We make sure the conversation is respectful and the artist isn't being pressured. But we are not a party to the agreement, we don't take a cut of the artist's fee, and the artist negotiates their own rate directly with the sponsor.
 
The artist can say no. Saying no does not affect the artist's relationship with Artist Safespaces or future engagements.
 
If a sponsor uses an artist's work outside the agreed scope, **we will go after them on the artist's behalf.** That's a commitment, not a gesture.
 
## 7. Plain-language agreements
 
Every contract we send is written so an artist without legal counsel can read it and understand exactly what it does. No "standard terms" hand-waving. No clauses described one way and operating another. If a term is in the agreement, it is explained in plain English.
 
If anything in a contract from us is unclear, we'll explain it before you sign — and we'll change it if it doesn't accurately reflect what we agreed to.
 
## 8. Termination is defined
 
Either party can exit a confirmed engagement with clear written notice. Kill fees are specified up front in every agreement. If Artist Safespaces cancels, the artist is paid for work completed plus a defined kill fee. If an artist cancels, we discuss a replacement plan and any pro-rated return of pre-payment.
 
We do not use ambiguity as leverage.
 
## 9. Sponsor terms do not override artist terms
 
If a sponsor wants something that conflicts with these standards — exclusivity, perpetual rights, work-for-hire, deferred payment — the sponsor adjusts or the deal does not happen.
 
These standards are the floor. They are not negotiable down to win a sponsorship.
 
---
 
## Why we publish this
 
The art world is full of "standard agreements" that quietly transfer authorship, control, and economic upside away from the people making the work. Artists are told to be grateful for opportunities and to read the fine print later, when it's already signed.
 
We're not interested in that model. We exist to build infrastructure for artists, which means our infrastructure has to start with how we treat them.
 
If you're an artist considering working with us: these are the terms before we ever talk. If you're a sponsor or partner: these are the constraints inside which we'll build something with you. Both groups deserve to know what they're walking into.
